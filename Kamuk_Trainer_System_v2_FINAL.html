Verb Tenses & Transitions
TensesThe 0/0/0 Method — Tense Coordination
What is the 0/0/0 Method?
The method treats every sentence as a combination lock: 3 positions that can rotate independently. Position 1 = Subject, Position 2 = Tense + Verb, Position 3 = Complement. The student only changes one position at a time — building automatic fluency like muscle memory.
0 / 0 / 0 Subject / Tense+Verb / Complement I / eat / breakfast I / ate / breakfast (past) I / am eating / breakfast (continuous) I / have eaten / breakfast (perfect)
Trainer: only one "0" changes at a time. Keep the other two identical. This trains automatic tense switching.
Exercise 1 — Tense Chain (same subject, same complement)
Trainer gives the subject and complement. Student must produce the same sentence in 5 tenses without stopping.
Base: I / [TENSE] / to the gym Present: I go to the gym. Past: I went to the gym. Future: I will go to the gym. Continuous: I am going to the gym. Perfect: I have gone to the gym.
Trainer: if the student pauses more than 3 seconds, give a hint: "past of GO?" — do not complete the sentence for them.
Exercise 2 — Anecdote with tense shift
Student writes a 15-minute anecdote that moves from past to present. Trainer marks every tense used and reviews during debate segment.
Topic: "Something you used to do as a child that you stopped and would like to do again." Expected tenses: used to / simple past / present simple / would like to
Routine 15+10: 15 min writing, 10 min debate. Correct each tense error immediately — student repeats the correct form before continuing.
Key irregular verbs to master first
come→came go→went take→took give→gave get→got make→made do→did say→said see→saw have→had be→was/were know→knew
Drill these 12 first. Most conversations use them constantly.
TensesTense Transitions — Shifting fluidly mid-story
Exercise — The Time Travel Story
Student tells a story that intentionally crosses 3 time periods. Trainer assigns the topic. Student must use at least 3 different tenses and signal each shift with a transition phrase.
Topic: "Tell me about something that happened at work / school, how it affected you then, and what you learned that you still apply today." Past (what happened) → Past continuous (what was happening) → Present (what you do now)
Transition phrases to teach: "Back then..." / "At that point..." / "Since then..." / "These days..." / "Looking back..."
Gonna / Used to / Would in past context
Gonna (past) = was going to / were going to "I was gonna call you but I forgot." Used to = habitual past action "I used to work nights — now I work days." Would = repeated past action "Every summer, we would drive to the beach."
These three are the most natural ways to talk about the past. Drill each one separately before combining.
Linkers, Connectors & Idea Expansion
LinkersIdea + Linker + Idea — The core pattern
The fundamental pattern
Every sentence the student produces must connect to the next one. No isolated sentences. The trainer marks every unconnected idea in red during writing exercises.
Idea → Linker → Idea → Linker → Idea "I enjoy working with people, and on top of that, I like solving problems. However, I do need some quiet time, because I tend to think better when I'm alone. That said, collaboration is something I genuinely value."
Goal: student should never produce a sentence without a connection to what came before or after.
Linker table — teach these groups one at a time
FunctionLinkers
Addingand, also, in addition, furthermore, moreover, as well as, on top of that, besides
Contrastingbut, however, although, even though, despite, in spite of, on the other hand, nevertheless
Giving reasonbecause, since, due to, owing to, as a result of, because of
Giving resultso, therefore, consequently, as a result, that's why, this means that
Sequencingfirst, then, after that, next, finally, lastly, to begin with
Summarizingin short, in brief, to summarize, in a nutshell, overall
Giving examplesfor example, for instance, such as, namely, like
Exercise — Linker Chain (15+10 Routine)
Trainer assigns a topic. Student writes for 15 minutes. Rule: every sentence must start OR end with a linker from the table above. Trainer counts linkers used in the debate review.
Topics: "Describe your job" / "Talk about your neighborhood" / "Explain your ideal weekend" Target: minimum 8 different linkers in 15 minutes of writing.
Start with Adding and Contrasting only. Add other groups week by week.
LinkersComplex idea creation — Expanding beyond simple sentences
The 0/0/0 Expansion Method
Take any simple idea and expand it by replacing or adding one "0" at a time.
Simple: I like music. Expand: I like listening to music / because it helps me focus. Expand: Most people enjoy music / especially when they're stressed. Expand: What I love about music / is that it can change your mood instantly.
Exercise — The "Why? How? What?" drill
Student makes a simple statement. Trainer asks "Why?" or "How?" or "What do you mean?" Student must expand without stopping. This is done orally — no writing.
Student: "I work in sales." Trainer: "What does that involve exactly?" Student: "Well, basically I'm responsible for finding new clients and maintaining the ones we already have, which means I spend a lot of time on calls and meetings..."
If student stops, trainer says "keep going" — not "that's fine." The goal is 60+ seconds of continuous speech on any topic.
Recovery Ability & Response Speed
RecoveryRecovery training — Never stop, always continue
The substitution technique
When a student doesn't know a word, they must use one of these substitution strategies instead of stopping. Train these phrases until they're automatic.
Strategy 1 — Describe it: "I don't remember the word, but it's something you use to..." Strategy 2 — Use a synonym: "It's like... / It's similar to... / It's the opposite of..." Strategy 3 — Give context: "You know when you... / It's the thing that happens when..." Strategy 4 — Buy time and continue: "How do I put this... / What I'm trying to say is... / Let me put it this way..."
3-second rule: if the student is silent for more than 3 seconds, trainer says "keep going" — never supply the word. Force substitution.
Exercise — The Interruption Drill
Student tells any story. Trainer interrupts at random moments with unexpected questions. Student must answer immediately and return to the story.
Student: "Last weekend I went to..." Trainer: "What do you usually do on Sundays?" Student: [must answer + return to story] Target: no silence longer than 3 seconds at any point.
RecoveryResponse speed — Reducing reaction time
Exercise — Rapid Fire Questions (5W)
Trainer asks 10 consecutive questions. Student must answer each one in under 5 seconds. No thinking time allowed.
What? / Where? / Who? / When? / Why? / How? How many? / How often? / How long? / What kind? "What's your favorite food?" — immediate answer "Why?" — immediate answer "How often do you eat it?" — immediate answer
The answer doesn't need to be perfect — it needs to be immediate. Fluency first, accuracy second at this stage.
Oral Structure & Organization
StructureOral structure — Beginning, development, close
The 3-part response structure
Opening: State your position / introduce the topic Development: Explain with examples and details Close: Summarize or give a conclusion "Well, the way I see it... [opening] This is because... / For example... [development] So overall, I think... / That's why..." [close]
For Emerging students: teach this through writing first. For Functional/Advanced: apply it directly in the debate segment.
STAR Method (for Advanced level)
S — Situation: Set the context. "I was working at..." T — Task: What was your responsibility? "My job was to..." A — Action: What did you do? "So what I did was..." R — Result: What happened? "As a result..."
Use STAR only with Functional-Advanced students. Emerging students need Idea+Linker+Idea foundation first.
StructureImprovisation under pressure
Exercise — The Random Topic Drill
Trainer gives a completely random topic. Student has 10 seconds to think and must speak for 60 seconds without stopping.
Topics: "Traffic" / "Your neighbor" / "Mondays" / "Coffee" / "Your phone" Structure to follow: 1. State something about the topic 2. Give an example or personal story 3. Connect to something bigger or give an opinion
Buying time phrases — teach before drilling improvisation
"That's a great question, let me think..." "Well, it depends on..." "To be honest with you..." "The way I see it..." "What I mean is..." "Honestly speaking..." "If I had to say..."
These phrases give the student 3–5 seconds to organize — which is all they need when the habit is built.
Natural Expressions, Contractions & Phrasal Verbs
ExpressionsNatural expressions — Sounding fluent, not textbook
High-frequency expressions to introduce first
Instead of "I think": "The way I see it" / "If you ask me" Instead of "For example": "Let's say..." / "Take [X] for instance" Instead of "But": "That said..." / "On the other hand..." Instead of "Also": "On top of that" / "Not only that, but..." Instead of "I don't know": "I'm not 100% sure, but..." Filler (natural): "You know what I mean?" / "Right?" / "Does that make sense?"
Introduce 3 per session. Student must use each one at least twice during the debate segment before moving on.
Synonyms — expanding vocabulary for recovery
Large → Big / Huge / Massive / Enormous Leave → Exit / Get out / Head out / Take off Problem → Issue / Challenge / Situation / Concern Good → Great / Solid / Strong / Solid / Decent Start → Begin / Kick off / Get started / Launch End → Finish / Wrap up / Close out / Conclude
ExpressionsContractions & natural speech — Gonna, Wanna, Gotta
Core contractions to naturalize speech
Gonna = going to → "I'm gonna call you later." Wanna = want to → "She wanna go home." Gotta = have got to → "I gotta finish this." Kinda = kind of → "It's kinda hard to explain." Sorta = sort of → "I sorta agree with you." PAST with Gonna: "I was gonna tell you but I forgot." "We were gonna go but it rained."
These are not informal — they are natural. A student who avoids them sounds robotic and over-formal in real work environments.
Common contractions in writing
I am → I'm You are → You're He is → He's They are → They're I will → I'll I would → I'd I have → I've I had → I'd Do not → Don't Can not → Can't Would not → Wouldn't Should not → Shouldn't
In writing exercises, trainer marks every long form and asks student to convert it. This builds automatic contraction use.
Prepositions & Grammar Support
GrammarPrepositions of time — AT / ON / IN / FOR / SINCE
Quick reference
AT → specific time: at 5pm, at midnight, at the moment ON → days & dates: on Monday, on July 14th, on Christmas Day IN → periods: in January, in 2021, in the morning, in winter FOR → duration: for 3 years, for two hours, for a week SINCE → start point: since 2019, since Monday, since I was a child DURING → within a period: during the meeting, during the film
Teach AT/ON/IN first as a set. Use the 0/0/0 method: "I [verb] [AT/ON/IN] [time]" — drill the three substituting only the preposition.
GrammarGerunds & Infinitives — The Endo-Ando rule
The Endo-Ando rule
In Spanish you can say "Yo comiendo" — in English you CANNOT say "I eating." You always need TO BE before the -ING form.
0 / 0 / 0 Pronoun / To Be / Verb+ING I / am / eating → Estoy comiendo She / is / running → Está corriendo They / are / working → Están trabajando He / was / watching → Estaba viendo
When student says "I eating" or "He running" — stop immediately. Say "To Be?" Student corrects: "I AM eating." This correction must be instant and consistent.
Session Routines
RoutineFoundations 15+10 — Emerging & Functional students
How to run the 15+10 routine
WRITING PHASE — 15 minutes • Trainer assigns topic (see topic bank below) • Student writes continuously — no stopping • Student must use at least 5 linkers • Student must use at least 2 different tenses • Trainer does NOT correct during writing — only observes DEBATE PHASE — 10 minutes • Student reads aloud what they wrote • Trainer corrects: tenses, linkers, structure, contractions • Each correction: student repeats the correct form ONCE before continuing • Trainer proposes 2-3 follow-up questions to extend the topic orally
Duration: run this routine for 1–2 months for Emerging students, 4 weeks for Functional. When the student no longer needs the writing phase to organize ideas, advance to full oral training.
Topic bank — Writing & debate topics by level
EMERGING TOPICS (simple, personal, familiar): • Describe your Monday routine • Talk about your neighborhood • What do you do on weekends? • Describe your family • What did you do last week? FUNCTIONAL TOPICS (require more structure): • Explain a challenge you faced at work/school and how you solved it • Talk about something you learned that changed the way you think • Describe your ideal job and why • What would you change about your city and how? ADVANCED TOPICS (opinion, analysis, debate): • Is social media more harmful or beneficial? Argue one side. • What skills do you think are most important for the next 10 years? • Talk about a leader you admire and what makes them effective
TensesPast tense activation — Students who only speak in present
Why students avoid past tense
Most students default to present tense because they're insecure about irregular verbs. The solution is not to drill irregular verbs in isolation — it's to force past tense use in context where the student has to communicate meaning.
Exercise — Yesterday Story: "Tell me everything you did yesterday from the moment you woke up." Student MUST use past tense for every action. Every present-tense error: trainer says "Yesterday, so...?" Student self-corrects immediately.
Target the 12 most common irregular verbs first: go/went, do/did, have/had, say/said, get/got, make/made, come/came, take/took, see/saw, know/knew, give/gave, be/was-were.
RecoveryComprehension at natural speed
Gradual speed exposure
Trainer gradually increases speaking speed each session. Student must respond without asking for repetition.
Week 1: Trainer speaks at 70% normal speed Week 2: 80% — student must understand and respond Week 3: 90% — closer to natural conversation speed Week 4+: 100% natural speed + natural fillers (uhh, you know, I mean...)
Also use: read authentic texts aloud to the student (news articles, etc.) at natural speed. Student summarizes what they understood without the text. This builds active listening under real conditions.
Philosophy — What this system is and isn't

This is not an English teaching program. It is an Operational English development system. The students in this program already know English — they have studied grammar, passed exams and can read and write. What they cannot do is operate in English under real-time professional conditions.

The goal is not to teach English. The goal is to activate the English already there and convert it into operational capacity: the ability to communicate, respond, solve problems and perform professionally in real conditions.

This means the trainer's job is not to explain grammar rules. The trainer's job is to push, correct in real time, create pressure and build habits. The student learns by doing — not by listening.

Core rule: the trainer never completes a sentence for the student. Never supplies the missing word. Never lets a silence go on for more than 3 seconds without saying "keep going." The student must always be the one to produce.
Student levels — what to expect and how to respond
Emerging — Score 11–15 / 25
Routine: Foundations 15+10 · Duration: 1–2 months
Can communicate with significant difficulty. Has knowledge but cannot operate it in real time. Confuses tenses, has no linkers, freezes when blocked. Requires the writing phase as structural scaffolding before any oral pressure is applied. Do not skip to debate-only sessions — the writing builds the framework the student's brain needs to organize before speaking.
Functional — Score 16–20 / 25
Routine: Operative Reinforcement 15+10 · Duration: 4 weeks
Can communicate with relative ease but has specific gaps in tense coordination, naturalness and structure. Knows the language well enough to feel confident — which is the danger. These students often resist correction because they feel they're communicating fine. The trainer must be precise: correct the gap, not the general performance.
Advanced — Score 21–25 / 25
Routine: Advanced Operational Training · 100% oral
Solid operational communicator. No writing phase needed. Training focuses on eliminating residual errors, increasing naturalness and preparing for STAR method and corporate simulation scenarios. The trainer's role shifts from corrector to sparring partner — pushing the student into increasingly complex and pressurized communication situations.
How to run a session — step by step
Emerging & Functional session (45 min)
1
Warm-up — 5 min
Start with 3 rapid-fire questions from last session's topic. This reactivates the language without pressure. Note any tense errors for later correction.
2
Writing phase — 15 min
Assign topic. Student writes continuously. Trainer observes silently — does not correct, does not intervene. Mark tense errors, missing linkers and structural problems on a separate sheet.
3
Debate phase — 10 min
Student reads aloud. Trainer corrects immediately on each error — student repeats the correct form before continuing. Trainer introduces 2–3 follow-up questions to push beyond the written text.
4
Expression bank — 5 min
Introduce 3 new linkers or expressions. Student uses each one in a sentence immediately. These go on the student's running list.
5
Feedback & next session — 5 min
Name the 2 most critical gaps from today's session. Set one specific focus for next session. Never list more than 2 things — the student needs focus, not a list of everything wrong.
Advanced session (45 min)
1
Random topic — 10 min
Trainer gives a topic with no warning. Student speaks for 60 seconds minimum without stopping. Trainer corrects residual errors in real time — student continues without restarting.
2
Simulation — 20 min
Role-play a professional scenario: job interview, difficult customer, team meeting, results presentation. Trainer acts the other role. Student must perform — not practice. The goal is zero preparation needed.
3
STAR debrief — 10 min
Trainer picks one moment from the simulation where the student's response was weak. Student re-does that response using full STAR structure: Situation → Task → Action → Result.
4
Feedback — 5 min
One thing done well. One thing to target next session. Done.
Correction protocol — how and when to correct
The correction rules
Rule 1: Correct immediately — not at the end. The brain connects correction to the moment of production. Delayed feedback is forgotten.
Rule 2: Student repeats the correct form ONCE before continuing. The trainer says the correct version — student repeats it — then continues. Never make the student restart the full sentence.
Rule 3: Correct maximum 3 things per session. More than that creates paralysis. Choose the 3 most critical gaps and focus there.
Rule 4: Never complete the student's sentence. Never supply the missing word. Trainer says "keep going" or "another way?" — the substitution must come from the student.
The trainer's silence is a tool. Let the student struggle for 3 seconds. That struggle is where the learning happens. Jumping in too fast removes the productive discomfort.
The 0/0/0 Method — trainer reference
How to use 0/0/0 in session

The 0/0/0 method is the structural backbone of the Infinity training system. Every sentence is three positions: Subject / Verb+Tense / Complement. The student rotates one position at a time — like a combination lock — building fluency through repetition of patterns, not memorization of phrases.

HOW TO DRILL IT IN SESSION: Step 1: Give the base pattern "I / like / listening to music" Step 2: Change only the 3rd position "I / like / [student fills in]" → cooking, reading, traveling, being alone... Step 3: Change only the 2nd position "Most people / [student fills in] / being alone" → hate, enjoy, avoid, can't stand, don't mind... Step 4: Change only the 1st position "[student fills in] / used to work late" → I, She, My parents, Most workers...
The student should never be told "wrong" — only "again" or "another one." The goal is volume of production, not perfection on the first try.
